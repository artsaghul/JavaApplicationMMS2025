Understanding Character Encodings: ASCII and Unicode
Character encodings are systems that allow computers to represent text (letters, numbers, and symbols) as binary data (1s and 0s). Two of the most significant standards in computing history are ASCII and Unicode.
1. ASCII (American Standard Code for Information Interchange)
Developed in the early 1960s, ASCII was the first major standard for character encoding.
Bit Depth: It originally used 7 bits to represent characters.
Capacity: It can represent 128 unique characters (2^7).
Character Set: Includes English letters (A-Z, a-z), digits (0-9), punctuation marks, and control characters (like "newline" or "backspace").
Limitation: ASCII is strictly limited to the English alphabet and basic symbols, making it insufficient for international communication.

2. Unicode
Unicode was created to solve the limitations of ASCII by providing a unique number for every character, no matter what the platform, device, application, or language.
Capacity: It can define over 1.1 million characters.
Character Set: Covers virtually all modern writing systems (Latin, Cyrillic, Arabic, Hebrew, Chinese, Japanese, etc.), as well as mathematical symbols, emojis, and historical scripts.
Relationship to ASCII: Unicode is designed to be backward compatible with ASCII. The first 128 characters of Unicode are identical to the ASCII set.
